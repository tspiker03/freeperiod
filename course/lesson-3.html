Lesson 3

Rubrics & Differentiation

Build clear, fair rubrics and adapt lessons for every learner — in seconds, not hours.

⏱ About 15 minutes

📏 Part 1: The /rubric Command

Writing rubrics is one of the most time-consuming parts of teaching. A good rubric has specific, observable criteria at every level. Vague language like "demonstrates understanding" doesn't help students or teachers.

Claude's /rubric command creates 4-level rubrics (Exceeding, Meeting, Approaching, Beginning) with concrete descriptors that tell students exactly what each level looks like.

🎯 Activity: Build a Rubric

1

Create a basic rubric

Try a rubric for an assignment you're currently using:

/rubric persuasive essay, 10th grade ELA, focus on thesis, evidence, and counterargument
2

Customize it

After seeing the first draft, refine it:

"Add a criteria row for grammar and mechanics, worth 10 points"
"Change the scale to 1-5 instead of the 4-level rubric"
"Make the language more student-friendly for 5th graders"
3

Try different assignment types

Rubrics aren't just for essays. Try:

/rubric science lab report, 8th grade, focus on hypothesis, procedure, data analysis, and conclusion
/rubric oral presentation, 6th grade, 3-5 minutes, focus on eye contact, voice projection, and content knowledge
/rubric group project poster, 4th grade social studies, topic is state geography

🎨 Part 2: The /differentiate Command

Every classroom has students working at different levels. The /differentiate command takes any lesson or activity and creates three tiers:

  • Approaching — scaffolded support for students who need it
  • Meeting — grade-level expectations
  • Exceeding — extension and enrichment for advanced learners

It also adds IEP/504 accommodations and ELL modifications automatically.

🚀 Activity: Differentiate a Lesson

4

Differentiate a lesson you've already made

If you created a lesson plan in Lesson 2, try differentiating it now:

/differentiate the lesson plan above

Or describe a lesson you're currently teaching:

/differentiate reading comprehension activity on "The Giver" chapter 5, 7th grade ELA
5

Ask for specific modifications

"Add sentence frames for the ELL students"
"Include a graphic organizer option for the approaching tier"
"The exceeding tier should include a choice board with 4 options"
6

Combine /rubric + /differentiate

This is powerful: after differentiating, ask Claude to adjust your rubric to match:

"Now modify the rubric so the 'approaching' tier criteria reflect the scaffolded version of the assignment"

💡 Pro Tips

  • Be specific about your students. "I have 3 ELL students at WIDA level 2" gets you better modifications than just "some ELL students."
  • Mention IEP goals. "One student has a writing IEP goal focused on paragraph structure" helps Claude tailor accommodations.
  • Ask for materials. "Create the graphic organizer you mentioned for the approaching tier" — Claude can make it.
  • Save good rubrics. Copy rubrics you like and reuse them as templates: "Use this rubric format but for a new assignment on [topic]."

🤔 Reflection

  • How do Claude's differentiation suggestions compare to what you usually do?
  • Were the IEP/504 accommodation suggestions relevant to your students?
  • What would you change or add to make the differentiated materials work in your classroom?
