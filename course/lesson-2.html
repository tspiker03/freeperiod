Lesson 2

Lesson Planning with /plan

Go beyond basic prompts. Learn to generate lesson plans that match your style, your standards, and your students.

⏱ About 15 minutes

🎯 What makes a good /plan prompt?

The more context you give Claude, the better the lesson plan. Here's the difference between a basic prompt and a great one:

/plan fractions 4th grade

This works, but it's generic. Compare with:

/plan adding fractions with unlike denominators, 4th grade math, 45-minute block, students have already learned equivalent fractions, align to NC 4.NF.3

The second prompt gives Claude the context it needs to produce something you can actually use tomorrow morning.

📝 Key details to include

You don't need all of these every time, but the more you provide, the better:

  • Topic and grade level — the basics
  • Class period length — "45-minute block" vs. "90-minute block"
  • Prior knowledge — what students already know
  • Standards — your state or Common Core standards
  • Special requirements — "no technology available" or "co-taught with SPED teacher"
  • Your style — "I like stations-based learning" or "include a gallery walk"

🚀 Activity: Plan a Real Lesson

Open Claude in your Teaching Assistant project and try each of these. Notice how the output changes with different prompts.

1

Plan a lesson for this week

Think of a topic you're actually teaching soon. Give Claude the details:

/plan [your topic] [grade level], [period length], students already know [prior knowledge], align to [your standards]
2

Ask Claude to modify it

After you get the first draft, try these follow-up prompts:

"Add a hands-on warm-up activity that takes 5 minutes"
"Replace the exit ticket with a think-pair-share"
"Make the independent practice a partner activity instead"
3

Try planning a unit

Claude can plan multiple days at once:

"Plan a 5-day unit on the water cycle for 6th grade science. Include a lab on day 3 and an assessment on day 5."

💡 Pro Tips

  • Iterate, don't restart. If the plan is 80% right, tell Claude what to change rather than starting over.
  • Ask for variations. "Give me two different warm-up options" lets you pick the one that fits.
  • Copy your school's template. Paste your school's lesson plan template into the chat and say "Use this format." Claude will follow it.
  • Combine commands. After generating a plan, type /differentiate to adapt it for different learners.

🤔 Reflection

After trying the activities above, consider:

  • What did Claude get right on the first try? What needed adjusting?
  • How does this compare to planning from scratch or adapting a template?
  • What details about your students or school context make the biggest difference in output quality?
